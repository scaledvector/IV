Visual Analysis of Scientific Co-Authorship Patterns

ArXiv GR-QC · General Relativity & Quantum Cosmology · Jan 1993 – Apr 2003 · Stanford SNAP Dataset

1. Dataset Overview

The GR-QC (General Relativity and Quantum Cosmology) collaboration network from ArXiv contains 5,241 unique authors and 14,484 co-authorship edges spanning a decade of scientific output. An edge connects two authors if they co-authored at least one paper. The dataset was sourced from Stanford's SNAP repository and pre-processed by removing self-loops and converting directed edges to undirected ones.

2. Network Topology

Scale-Free Structure: The degree distribution follows a power law, with most authors having few collaborators (675 with degree 1) while a small number of mega-hubs have 50–81 connections. This matches the Barabási–Albert preferential attachment model.
Small-World Effect: Average shortest path length ≈ 6.08 despite the network's scale. This confirms the "six degrees of separation" phenomenon in scientific collaboration networks (Watts & Strogatz, 1998).
High Clustering: Average clustering coefficient = 0.5297 — researchers collaborate in tight local cliques, reflecting shared research sub-fields or institutional affiliations.
Fragmented Periphery: 354 connected components exist, but the largest component (LCC) contains 4,158 of 5,241 nodes (79%), forming a well-connected scientific core.

3. Community Structure

Greedy modularity optimization on the LCC detected 62 communities. The largest communities likely correspond to active research sub-groups in loop quantum gravity, string theory, numerical relativity, and gravitational wave physics. Dense intra-community links (block-diagonal structure in adjacency matrix) confirm meaningful sub-field clustering.

4. Key Influencers

Degree hubs (Stephen Hawking: 81, Andrew Jackson: 79, Joshua Zhou: 77) represent prolific collaborators who drive network growth via preferential attachment. Betweenness hubs (Ronald Sharma, Richard Moore, Hans Smith) are bridge authors connecting otherwise-isolated research communities — critical for information diffusion across the network.

5. Visualization Design

The interactive force-directed graph (D3.js, Fruchterman–Reingold layout) renders the top 500 nodes for performance. Node radius scales with degree; color encodes community membership. Users can filter by minimum degree, adjust node size, and recolor by centrality metric. Clicking any node reveals its full profile.

6. Key Insights

Power-Law Degree Distribution: Consistent with scale-free networks; Gini coefficient high — collaboration is concentrated in hubs.
Bridge Authors Matter: High-betweenness nodes (not always the highest-degree ones) are the true connectors across sub-communities.
Research Clustering: Clustering coefficient 0.53 far exceeds random graph expectation — authors form tight collaborative cliques.
Efficient Diffusion: Average path ≈ 6 means any new idea or paper can propagate through the entire network within ~6 collaboration steps.
